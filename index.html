01

Enzymes: The Catalysts of Life

Every chemical reaction in your body, from digesting food to copying DNA, would be impossibly slow at body temperature without help. That help comes from enzymes, biological catalysts that speed up reactions without being consumed in the process. Nearly all enzymes are proteins (though a few are RNA molecules called ribozymes). They work by lowering the activation energy of a reaction, which is the energy "hump" that molecules must get over before a reaction can proceed. The reaction still releases or absorbs the same total energy; the enzyme simply makes it easier to get started.

Think of activation energy like pushing a boulder over a hill. Without an enzyme, the hill is tall and the push is enormous. The enzyme carves a lower pass through the hill so the boulder rolls over with much less effort. The starting point and ending point are the same; only the path changes.

Example
Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) breaks down into water and oxygen on its own, but extremely slowly. The enzyme catalase in your cells speeds this reaction up by a factor of about 10 million. A single catalase molecule can decompose millions of H₂O₂ molecules per second.
Key Takeaway
Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up reactions by lowering activation energy. They are not used up and do not change the overall energy balance of the reaction.
Common Mistake
Enzymes do NOT add energy to a reaction or change whether a reaction is energetically favorable. They only lower the activation energy barrier so the reaction happens faster.
TermDefinition
EnzymeA biological catalyst, usually a protein, that speeds up a chemical reaction.
CatalystA substance that accelerates a reaction without being permanently altered.
Activation EnergyThe minimum energy input needed to start a chemical reaction.
SubstrateThe specific molecule(s) an enzyme acts upon.
ProductThe molecule(s) that result from an enzymatic reaction.
02

How Enzymes Work

Each enzyme has a specially shaped pocket called the active site where the substrate binds. The shape of the active site is complementary to the shape of the substrate, which is why enzymes are highly specific: a given enzyme typically catalyzes only one reaction or acts on only one type of molecule. This specificity was originally described as a lock-and-key model (the substrate fits the enzyme like a key in a lock), but the more accurate picture is the induced-fit model. In induced fit, when the substrate approaches the active site, both the enzyme and substrate change shape slightly to create a tighter, more effective binding. It is less like a rigid lock and more like a handshake, where both hands adjust to grip each other.

Once the substrate is bound, the enzyme stabilizes the transition state of the reaction, lowers the activation energy, and the substrate is converted to product. The product then detaches, and the enzyme is free to accept another substrate molecule. This cycle can repeat thousands or even millions of times per second, a rate measured as the enzyme's turnover number.

Example
The digestive enzyme sucrase breaks the disaccharide sucrose (table sugar) into glucose and fructose. Sucrase's active site is shaped to bind sucrose specifically. It will not break down lactose or maltose because those sugars have different shapes.
Key Takeaway
Enzymes bind substrates at the active site with high specificity. The induced-fit model describes how both enzyme and substrate adjust shape for optimal binding.
Common Mistake
The lock-and-key model suggests a rigid, perfect fit. Exams often ask for the more modern model: induced fit, which accounts for the flexible shape changes that occur during binding.
TermDefinition
Active SiteThe specific region on an enzyme where the substrate binds and the reaction occurs.
Lock-and-Key ModelThe older model proposing that the enzyme's active site is a rigid, exact complement to the substrate.
Induced-Fit ModelThe current model: both enzyme and substrate change shape slightly upon binding for a tighter fit.
Enzyme-Substrate ComplexThe temporary structure formed when the substrate is bound in the enzyme's active site.
Turnover NumberThe number of substrate molecules one enzyme can convert to product per second.
03

Enzyme Regulation

Cells do not want every reaction running at full speed all the time. They need to turn enzymes up or down depending on conditions. Several factors influence enzyme activity. Temperature affects the rate: warming up generally speeds reactions, but too much heat denatures (unfolds) the protein, destroying the active site. Each enzyme has an optimal temperature where it works best. Similarly, pH affects enzyme shape, and each enzyme has an optimal pH. Pepsin in your stomach works best at a very acidic pH of about 2, while trypsin in the small intestine prefers a pH near 8.

Beyond temperature and pH, cells use molecular switches. A competitive inhibitor is a molecule that resembles the substrate and binds directly in the active site, blocking the real substrate. A noncompetitive inhibitor binds to a different part of the enzyme (an allosteric site), changing the enzyme's shape so the active site no longer works properly. Cells also use allosteric regulation positively: an activator molecule binds to an allosteric site and improves the enzyme's function. Finally, feedback inhibition is a common control strategy where the end product of a metabolic pathway inhibits an enzyme earlier in the pathway, preventing overproduction.

Example
Imagine an assembly line making toy cars. If you have too many toy cars stacking up at the end, a worker at the beginning of the line gets a signal to slow down or stop. That is feedback inhibition: the final product tells an early enzyme to reduce activity.
Key Takeaway
Enzyme activity depends on temperature, pH, and the presence of inhibitors or activators. Feedback inhibition is a key self-regulation strategy in metabolic pathways.
Common Mistake
Competitive inhibitors bind at the active site and CAN be overcome by adding more substrate. Noncompetitive inhibitors bind elsewhere and cannot be overcome by adding more substrate. Do not mix these up on exams.
TermDefinition
DenaturationThe unfolding of a protein due to heat, extreme pH, or other stress, destroying its function.
Optimal Temperature/pHThe temperature or pH at which an enzyme functions most efficiently.
Competitive InhibitorA molecule that blocks the active site by mimicking the substrate's shape.
Noncompetitive InhibitorA molecule that binds to an allosteric site, changing enzyme shape so the active site cannot function.
Allosteric SiteA regulatory site on an enzyme, distinct from the active site, where inhibitors or activators bind.
Feedback InhibitionA pathway's end product inhibits an early enzyme, preventing overproduction.
04

Glycolysis: Splitting Glucose

Now that we understand enzymes, we can follow how cells actually harvest energy from food. The first step, shared by virtually every living cell on Earth, is glycolysis (literally "sugar splitting"). Glycolysis takes place in the cytoplasm, not inside any organelle, and it does not require oxygen. It is a sequence of 10 enzyme-catalyzed reactions that breaks one 6-carbon glucose molecule into two 3-carbon molecules of pyruvate.

Glycolysis has two phases. The energy investment phase (first 5 steps) actually uses 2 ATP to get started, like investing money to make money. The energy payoff phase (last 5 steps) produces 4 ATP and 2 NADH. The net gain is therefore 2 ATP and 2 NADH per glucose. That is a modest yield compared to what comes later in aerobic respiration, but glycolysis is fast and does not need oxygen, which makes it essential for cells in low-oxygen environments.

Example
During an intense sprint, your muscle cells cannot get oxygen fast enough. They rely heavily on glycolysis for quick ATP production. This is why sprinting can only be sustained for a short time: glycolysis alone does not yield much ATP.
Key Takeaway
Glycolysis splits one glucose into two pyruvate in the cytoplasm, producing a net gain of 2 ATP and 2 NADH. It does not require oxygen.
Common Mistake
Students often say glycolysis produces 4 ATP. Technically it does, but 2 ATP were invested at the start. Always report the NET gain: 2 ATP.
TermDefinition
GlycolysisA 10-step metabolic pathway in the cytoplasm that splits glucose into two pyruvate molecules.
PyruvateThe 3-carbon molecule produced as the end product of glycolysis.
NAD⁺ / NADHAn electron carrier; NAD⁺ is the oxidized form, NADH is the reduced (electron-loaded) form.
Net ATP YieldThe actual ATP profit after subtracting ATP invested. For glycolysis: 2 ATP per glucose.
AnaerobicA process that does not require oxygen.
05

Fermentation: Life Without Oxygen

When oxygen is absent or scarce, the cell has a problem: glycolysis produces NADH, but without oxygen there is no way to "recycle" NADH back to NAD⁺ through the electron transport chain. If NAD⁺ runs out, glycolysis grinds to a halt. Fermentation solves this by providing an alternative way to regenerate NAD⁺ from NADH, allowing glycolysis to keep running.

There are two main types. In lactic acid fermentation, pyruvate is converted directly to lactate (lactic acid), and NADH gives up its electrons to become NAD⁺ again. This happens in your muscle cells during intense exercise and also in certain bacteria used to make yogurt. In alcohol (ethanol) fermentation, pyruvate is first converted to acetaldehyde (releasing CO₂), and then acetaldehyde is reduced to ethanol while NADH is recycled to NAD⁺. Yeast cells use this pathway, which is why yeast produces alcohol and CO₂ bubbles in bread and beer.

Importantly, fermentation itself does not produce any additional ATP. The only ATP comes from glycolysis (net 2 ATP). Fermentation's sole purpose is to regenerate NAD⁺ so glycolysis can continue.

Example
When you exercise hard and your muscles burn, that burning sensation is associated with lactate buildup from lactic acid fermentation. Your muscles switch to fermentation when oxygen delivery cannot keep up with ATP demand. When you catch your breath, oxygen returns and aerobic respiration resumes.
Key Takeaway
Fermentation does not produce extra ATP. Its purpose is to recycle NADH back to NAD⁺ so glycolysis can keep generating 2 ATP per glucose in the absence of oxygen.
Common Mistake
"Fermentation produces ATP" is misleading. The ATP comes from glycolysis, not from fermentation reactions. Fermentation only regenerates NAD⁺.
TermDefinition
FermentationAn anaerobic process that regenerates NAD⁺ from NADH so glycolysis can continue.
Lactic Acid FermentationPyruvate is reduced to lactate; occurs in muscle cells and some bacteria.
Alcohol FermentationPyruvate is converted to ethanol and CO₂; occurs in yeast.
LactateThe product of lactic acid fermentation; a 3-carbon molecule.
EthanolThe 2-carbon alcohol produced in alcohol fermentation.
06

Aerobic Respiration: The Full Harvest

When oxygen IS available, cells can extract far more energy from glucose. After glycolysis, each pyruvate enters the mitochondrion and is converted into acetyl-CoA (a 2-carbon group attached to coenzyme A), releasing one CO₂ and one NADH per pyruvate. Acetyl-CoA then enters the citric acid cycle (also called the Krebs cycle or TCA cycle), a circular series of reactions in the mitochondrial matrix.

In each turn of the citric acid cycle, the 2-carbon acetyl group is completely broken down into 2 CO₂, and the energy is captured as 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, and 1 GTP (equivalent to 1 ATP). Since each glucose produces 2 pyruvate and therefore 2 acetyl-CoA, the cycle turns twice per glucose. Adding it all up through the citric acid cycle (not yet counting the electron transport chain), glucose has been fully oxidized to CO₂, and the cell holds a collection of electron carriers (NADH and FADH₂) loaded with energy.

Example
Think of the citric acid cycle like wringing out a wet towel. Each twist (reaction) squeezes out a bit more energy (as NADH or FADH₂) until the towel (carbon from glucose) is completely dry (released as CO₂).
Key Takeaway
The citric acid cycle in the mitochondrial matrix fully breaks down acetyl-CoA to CO₂ while loading electron carriers (NADH, FADH₂) with energy for the next stage.
Common Mistake
The citric acid cycle itself produces very little ATP directly (just 1 GTP/ATP per turn). The big ATP payoff comes in the next step, the electron transport chain. Do not overstate the cycle's direct ATP yield.
TermDefinition
Acetyl-CoAA 2-carbon molecule linked to coenzyme A; the fuel that enters the citric acid cycle.
Citric Acid CycleA cycle of reactions in the mitochondrial matrix that oxidizes acetyl-CoA to CO₂, generating NADH, FADH₂, and GTP.
Krebs Cycle / TCA CycleAlternate names for the citric acid cycle.
FADH₂An electron carrier that delivers electrons to the electron transport chain (lower energy than NADH).
Mitochondrial MatrixThe inner compartment of the mitochondrion where the citric acid cycle occurs.
07

The Electron Transport Chain & ATP Synthase

This is where the big ATP payoff happens. The NADH and FADH₂ generated by glycolysis, pyruvate oxidation, and the citric acid cycle carry high-energy electrons to the electron transport chain (ETC), a series of protein complexes embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane. As electrons are passed from one complex to the next (like a bucket brigade), they release energy at each step. That energy is used to pump hydrogen ions (H⁺) from the matrix into the intermembrane space, creating a concentration gradient, a kind of molecular "dam" of protons.

The protons then flow back into the matrix through a special channel protein called ATP synthase. As H⁺ ions rush through ATP synthase, it physically spins like a tiny turbine, and this rotation drives the attachment of a phosphate group to ADP, producing ATP. This mechanism is called oxidative phosphorylation because oxygen is the final electron acceptor at the end of the chain (combining with electrons and H⁺ to form water), and the phosphorylation of ADP is powered by the proton gradient. The overall yield of aerobic respiration is approximately 30-32 ATP per glucose, compared to just 2 from glycolysis alone.

Example
Imagine a hydroelectric dam. Water (H⁺ ions) builds up behind the dam (inner membrane). When the floodgates open (ATP synthase), the rushing water spins a turbine (the rotary part of ATP synthase) that generates electricity (ATP). Oxygen at the end of the chain is like the river downstream, accepting the spent water.
Key Takeaway
The electron transport chain creates a proton gradient; ATP synthase uses that gradient to produce most of the cell's ATP (~30-32 per glucose). Oxygen is the final electron acceptor.
Common Mistake
Without oxygen, the ETC stops because there is nothing to accept the electrons at the end. This is precisely why we need to breathe: oxygen keeps the ETC (and therefore ATP production) running.
TermDefinition
Electron Transport ChainA series of membrane proteins that pass electrons, using the released energy to pump H⁺ ions.
ATP SynthaseA channel/enzyme that uses the H⁺ gradient to synthesize ATP as protons flow through it.
Oxidative PhosphorylationATP production driven by the electron transport chain and chemiosmosis.
ChemiosmosisThe flow of H⁺ ions down their gradient through ATP synthase, powering ATP synthesis.
Proton GradientThe difference in H⁺ concentration across the inner mitochondrial membrane that stores potential energy.
08

Photosynthesis: Capturing Light

Photosynthesis is essentially the reverse of cellular respiration. Instead of breaking down glucose to release energy, photosynthesis uses light energy to build glucose from CO₂ and water. The overall equation is: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂. This process occurs in chloroplasts, organelles found in plant cells and algae. Inside the chloroplast, you find stacks of membrane discs called thylakoids (the stacks are called grana) and a fluid-filled space surrounding them called the stroma.

Photosynthesis has two main stages. The light-dependent reactions happen in the thylakoid membranes. Pigment molecules, primarily chlorophyll, absorb photons of light. This energy excites electrons, which are passed through an electron transport chain in the thylakoid membrane (similar in concept to the mitochondrial ETC). The energy is used to pump H⁺ into the thylakoid interior, and ATP synthase then produces ATP as H⁺ flows back out. Water molecules are split to replace the lost electrons, releasing O₂ as a byproduct. The excited electrons also reduce NADP⁺ to NADPH. So the light reactions produce ATP and NADPH, which power the next stage.

Example
Leaves are green because chlorophyll absorbs red and blue light but reflects green light. If a plant is grown under only green light, it cannot photosynthesize well because chlorophyll barely absorbs green wavelengths.
Key Takeaway
The light reactions in the thylakoid membranes use sunlight to split water, produce O₂, and generate ATP and NADPH for the Calvin cycle.
Common Mistake
The oxygen released in photosynthesis comes from water (H₂O), NOT from CO₂. Water is split by the light reactions, and the oxygen atoms leave as O₂ gas.
TermDefinition
ChloroplastThe organelle in plant and algal cells where photosynthesis occurs.
ThylakoidA membrane disc inside the chloroplast where light-dependent reactions occur.
Granum (pl. Grana)A stack of thylakoid membranes.
StromaThe fluid-filled space surrounding the thylakoids in the chloroplast.
ChlorophyllThe primary green pigment that absorbs light energy in photosynthesis.
NADPHAn electron carrier produced in the light reactions, used to power the Calvin cycle.
Light-Dependent ReactionsThe stage of photosynthesis that requires light; produces ATP, NADPH, and O₂.
09

The Calvin Cycle: Building Sugar

The second stage of photosynthesis is the Calvin cycle (also called the light-independent reactions or "dark reactions," though this name is misleading because it does not require darkness; it simply does not directly use light). The Calvin cycle takes place in the stroma of the chloroplast and uses the ATP and NADPH from the light reactions to convert CO₂ into sugar.

The cycle has three main phases. First, carbon fixation: the enzyme RuBisCO attaches CO₂ to a 5-carbon sugar called RuBP, producing two 3-carbon molecules (3-phosphoglycerate, or 3-PGA). Second, reduction: ATP and NADPH are used to convert 3-PGA into G3P (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate), a 3-carbon sugar. Third, regeneration: most of the G3P is recycled to regenerate RuBP so the cycle can continue. After three turns of the cycle (fixing 3 CO₂), one net G3P molecule exits, and two G3P molecules can be combined to make one glucose.

Example
RuBisCO is the most abundant enzyme on Earth because every plant on the planet uses it for carbon fixation. It is estimated that RuBisCO fixes about 100 billion tons of carbon from the atmosphere each year.
Key Takeaway
The Calvin cycle uses ATP and NADPH from the light reactions to fix CO₂ into sugar (G3P) in the stroma. RuBisCO is the key enzyme for carbon fixation.
Common Mistake
"Dark reactions" does NOT mean the Calvin cycle happens only in the dark. It happens during the day, alongside the light reactions. It just does not directly require photons of light.
TermDefinition
Calvin CycleThe cycle of reactions in the stroma that uses ATP and NADPH to fix CO₂ into G3P sugar.
Carbon FixationThe incorporation of CO₂ into an organic molecule (catalyzed by RuBisCO).
RuBisCOThe enzyme that catalyzes carbon fixation; the most abundant protein on Earth.
G3PGlyceraldehyde-3-phosphate; the 3-carbon sugar product of the Calvin cycle.
Light-Independent ReactionsAnother name for the Calvin cycle; does not directly require light.

Cytoskeletal Systems

The cytoskeleton is a dynamic network of protein filaments that gives the cell its shape, provides mechanical support, organizes internal structures, and enables movement. It is not a fixed scaffold; it is constantly being assembled and disassembled as the cell's needs change. There are three main types of cytoskeletal filaments, each made of different proteins and serving different roles.

Microfilaments (Actin Filaments)

Microfilaments are the thinnest filaments, made of the protein actin. They are concentrated just beneath the cell membrane and are responsible for cell shape changes, crawling movement, and the pinching that occurs during cytokinesis (when a dividing cell splits in two). Actin filaments are dynamic: they can polymerize (grow) at one end and depolymerize (shrink) at the other, a process called treadmilling.

Intermediate Filaments

Intermediate filaments are medium-thickness filaments made of various fibrous proteins (like keratin in skin cells and vimentin in connective tissue). They are the toughest and most stable of the three types and act as the cell's "ropes," providing mechanical strength and anchoring organelles in place. Unlike microtubules and microfilaments, intermediate filaments are not polar and do not serve as tracks for motor proteins.

Microtubules

Microtubules are the thickest filaments, made of the protein tubulin (alpha and beta subunits). They radiate outward from the centrosome (the main microtubule-organizing center near the nucleus) and serve as highways for intracellular transport. Motor proteins carry cargo along microtubules. During cell division, microtubules form the mitotic spindle that separates chromosomes. Microtubules also form the structural core of cilia and flagella.

Example
Think of the cytoskeleton like the infrastructure of a city. Microfilaments are the sidewalks and small paths (flexible, near the surface). Intermediate filaments are the steel cables of suspension bridges (strong, durable). Microtubules are the highways and rail lines (major transport routes for moving cargo).
Key Takeaway
Microfilaments (actin) handle shape and movement, intermediate filaments provide mechanical strength, and microtubules (tubulin) serve as transport highways and form the mitotic spindle.
Common Mistake
Students often confuse the three filament types. Remember the size order: microfilaments are thinnest, intermediate filaments are medium, microtubules are thickest. Associate each with its protein: actin, various (keratin, etc.), tubulin.
TermDefinition
MicrofilamentThin actin filaments involved in cell shape, movement, and cytokinesis.
ActinThe protein that polymerizes to form microfilaments.
Intermediate FilamentMedium, rope-like filaments (e.g., keratin) providing mechanical strength.
MicrotubuleThick, hollow tubes of tubulin used for transport, structure, and chromosome separation.
TubulinThe protein subunit (alpha and beta) that polymerizes to form microtubules.
CentrosomeThe main microtubule-organizing center of the cell, located near the nucleus.
TreadmillingThe dynamic process of adding subunits at one end while removing them at the other.

Cellular Movement: Motility & Contractility

Cells are not static; many types of cells move, and internal movement is constant in virtually all cells. This section covers the molecular machinery that makes movement possible.

Motor Proteins

Motor proteins are enzymes that convert chemical energy (ATP) into mechanical work. The three main families are kinesin, dynein, and myosin. Kinesin walks along microtubules toward the cell periphery (the "plus end"), carrying vesicles and organelles outward. Dynein walks along microtubules toward the centrosome (the "minus end"), moving cargo inward. Myosin interacts with actin filaments and is best known for its role in muscle contraction, but it also participates in cell crawling and cytokinesis.

Cilia and Flagella

Cilia are short, hair-like projections that beat in coordinated waves. Cells lining your respiratory tract use cilia to sweep mucus and debris upward and out of your lungs. Flagella are longer, whip-like appendages; a human sperm cell uses a single flagellum to swim. Both cilia and flagella have an internal structure called the axoneme, built from a ring of nine microtubule doublets surrounding a central pair (the "9+2" arrangement). Dynein arms between the doublets slide the microtubules past each other, and because the microtubules are anchored at the base, sliding is converted into bending.

Muscle Contraction (Sliding Filament Model)

Skeletal muscle is the most dramatic example of cell motility. Muscle cells (fibers) contain repeating units called sarcomeres, the basic contractile units. Each sarcomere contains overlapping thick filaments (made of myosin) and thin filaments (made of actin). During contraction, myosin heads bind to actin, undergo a power stroke that pulls the actin filaments inward, then release and re-cock. This cycle, powered by ATP, slides the thin filaments past the thick filaments, shortening the sarcomere and generating force. This is the sliding filament model.

Example
Kinesin walking along a microtubule looks remarkably like a person walking. It has two "feet" (heads) that take alternating steps along the microtubule track, each step powered by hydrolyzing one ATP molecule. A single kinesin can carry a vesicle hundreds of nanometers across the cell.
Key Takeaway
Motor proteins (kinesin, dynein, myosin) convert ATP into movement. Cilia and flagella use dynein-driven microtubule sliding. Muscle contraction uses the sliding filament model of actin and myosin.
Common Mistake
Kinesin and dynein both walk on microtubules but in opposite directions: kinesin toward the plus end (outward), dynein toward the minus end (inward). Myosin walks on actin, not microtubules. Do not mix up the tracks.
TermDefinition
Motor ProteinA protein that uses ATP to produce mechanical movement along cytoskeletal filaments.
KinesinMotor protein that moves cargo toward the plus end of microtubules (outward).
DyneinMotor protein that moves cargo toward the minus end of microtubules (inward); also powers cilia/flagella.
MyosinMotor protein that interacts with actin; central to muscle contraction.
CiliaShort, numerous projections that beat in waves; used for moving fluids past cell surfaces.
FlagellumA long, whip-like appendage used for propulsion (e.g., sperm cells).
AxonemeThe 9+2 microtubule arrangement forming the core of cilia and flagella.
SarcomereThe basic contractile unit of muscle, containing overlapping actin and myosin filaments.
Sliding Filament ModelThe mechanism of muscle contraction where myosin pulls actin filaments inward, shortening the sarcomere.
